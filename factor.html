Select the top ten and last ten countries in the happiness index in 2019, and visualize their GDP per Capita Comparison, Health Life Expectancy Comparison, Freedom to Make Life Choice Comparison, Perceptions of Corruption Comparison, Generosity Comparison from 2015 to 2019.

As can be seen from the figure, in 2019, the top ten countries in the happiness index are Finland, Denmark, Norway, Iceland, Netherlands, Switzerland, Sweden, New, Zealand, Canada and Austria. They all scored above 7.2, and the highest score was Finland, reaching nearly 7.77 points. The last ten countries in the happiness index are Haiti, Botswana, Syria, Malawi, Yemen, Rwanda, Tanzania, Afghanistan, Central African Republic and South Sudan. Their scores are all less than 3.6. This is nearly half the gap with countries with high levels of happiness.

It can be seen from the above two graphs that the GDP per Capita growth trend of the top 10 countries is close to the same, showing an upward trend from 2015 to 2017, a decline from 2017 to 2018, and then a rebound trend in 2019. The GDP per Capita trend of the last 10 countries is not regular, and the score gap is large. For example, Botswana showed an upward trend from 2015 to 2017, while the Central African Republic showed a downward trend in these three years.

The Health Life Trend in last10 countries is different. But in 2018 to 2019, Health Life Expectancy is on the rise trend in nine countries, with the exception of Syria.

From the figure, the Freedom to Make Life Choice trend in top 10 countries is similar and the scores are similar. It showed a downward trend in 2015 to 2016, an upward trend in 2016-18 and a downward trend in 2018 to 2019. Although the last 10 countries have large differences in scores, most countries have had the same trend in The Freedom to Make Life Choice in recent years, that is, decline first, then rise, then fall.

From the chart, it can be seen that the top 10 countries have different trends in Perceptions of Corruption, and there is a large gap between the scores of different countries. But the overall trend is flattening out. Nine of the last 10 countries had Perceptions of Corruption scores between 0 and 0.2 over the years, with the exception of Rwanda. Rwanda's Perceptions of Corruption scores have fallen sharply over the years.

As can be seen from the figure, the score of the top 10 countries and the last 10 countries showed a similar trend from 2015 to 2019 in Generosity. It showed a slow upward trend from 2015 to 2017, and a downward trend from 2017 to 2018. It rose from 2018 to 2019, but the final score was not as high as that of 2015. At the same time, it can be seen that the scores of most of the last 10 countries are not significantly different from those of the top 10 countries.
